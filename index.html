Check Out the Resorts

When to Go

Japan's powder season runs December through March, but there's a sweet spot for the best snow with fewer crowds.

Best Window

Mid-January — Mid-February

Peak powder season. Consistent snowfall, cold temperatures, and the deepest base depths. This is when Japan earns its "Japow" reputation — expect regular dumps of light, dry powder.

Avoid

Busy Periods

  • Chinese New Year (late Jan / early Feb) — Niseko especially gets packed
  • Australian School Holidays (late Dec — late Jan) — Niseko and Hakuba see big Aussie crowds
  • Japanese Public Holidays — Coming of Age Day (mid-Jan), National Foundation Day (Feb 11)
Our Pick

Late January — Early February 2026

Targeting the last week of January or first week of February gives you peak conditions while dodging the worst of the holiday crowds. Aim for a Saturday-to-Saturday window for the best flight options.

The Resorts

Four world-class options, each with a different flavour. Here's how they stack up.

Niseko

Hokkaido
15m+Annual snowfall
887mVertical drop
4Linked resorts

The most famous powder destination in Japan. Four interconnected resorts (Grand Hirafu, Hanazono, Niseko Village, Annupuri) offer huge terrain variety. English-friendly with an excellent après scene.

Pros

  • Legendary consistent powder
  • Great nightlife and restaurants
  • Easy for English speakers
  • Large skiable area with night skiing

Cons

  • Most expensive option
  • Can feel less "authentically Japanese"
  • Gets crowded in peak weeks

Lift Pass

~¥7,500/day (~$70 AUD)

Hakuba

Nagano, Japanese Alps
11m+Annual snowfall
1,071mVertical drop
10Resorts in valley

Host of the 1998 Winter Olympics. Hakuba Valley has 10 resorts offering everything from steep alpine terrain to mellow groomers. A great mix of Japanese culture and international amenities.

Pros

  • Biggest vertical drop in Japan
  • Huge variety of terrain across 10 resorts
  • Good balance of culture and convenience
  • Easier access from Tokyo (bus or train)

Cons

  • Snow can be heavier/wetter than Hokkaido
  • Resorts aren't all linked — need transport between some
  • Can get windy at higher elevations

Lift Pass

~¥6,200/day (~$58 AUD)

Myoko Kogen

Niigata
14m+Annual snowfall
760mVertical drop
5Resorts in area

One of Japan's best-kept secrets. Myoko gets dumped on consistently and sees far fewer international tourists. The authentic Japanese ski town experience with incredible onsens and local food.

Pros

  • Massive snowfall — rivals Niseko
  • Way fewer crowds
  • Authentic Japanese mountain town
  • Excellent value for money

Cons

  • Limited English signage/menus
  • Smaller ski areas individually
  • Fewer nightlife options

Lift Pass

~¥5,000/day (~$47 AUD)

Nozawa Onsen

Nagano
12m+Annual snowfall
1,085mVertical drop
13Free public onsens

A charming traditional village built around natural hot springs. The skiing is excellent with great tree runs and steep terrain up top, and the village itself is one of the most atmospheric in Japan.

Pros

  • Beautiful traditional village atmosphere
  • 13 free public onsens
  • Great tree skiing and off-piste
  • Big vertical — one of Japan's largest single resorts

Cons

  • Single resort — less variety day-to-day
  • Village can be hilly to walk around
  • Limited accommodation for big groups

Lift Pass

~¥5,500/day (~$52 AUD)

Getting There

Where you fly depends on which resort you pick. Here's the breakdown.

For Niseko

Your City → New Chitose (CTS), Sapporo

Direct flights available from Sydney, and various Asian hubs. From New Chitose Airport, it's a 2.5-hour bus or private transfer to Niseko.

Flights: ~$800–1,400 AUD return

For Hakuba / Nozawa / Myoko

Your City → Tokyo (NRT/HND)

Fly into Tokyo, then transfer:

  • Hakuba: 4hr bus from Shinjuku or 3.5hr via bullet train + local train
  • Nozawa Onsen: 2.5hr bullet train (Hokuriku Shinkansen) to Iiyama, then 25min bus
  • Myoko Kogen: 2hr bullet train (Joetsu Shinkansen) to Joetsumyoko, then 30min bus

Flights: ~$700–1,200 AUD return

Pro Tips

  • Book a JR Pass if doing Honshu resorts — covers bullet trains and saves money
  • Consider a night in Tokyo on arrival to recover from the flight before heading to the snow
  • Ski bags fly free on most airlines to Japan — check your airline's policy
  • Set up flight alerts on Google Flights or Skyscanner early for the best deals

Where to Stay

Prices are per person per night based on a group of 6-8 splitting costs.

Niseko

Chalet / Lodge ~$80–150 pp/night

Best for groups. Private chalet with kitchen, living area, drying room. Book early — popular ones go fast.

Hotel ~$100–200 pp/night

Ski-in/ski-out options available. More amenities but less group atmosphere.

Hakuba

Lodge / Pension ~$50–100 pp/night

Great value lodges run by locals. Often include breakfast and dinner. Cozy group vibe.

Chalet ~$60–120 pp/night

Self-catering chalets throughout the valley. Good for groups who want independence.

Myoko Kogen

Ryokan (Traditional Inn) ~$60–100 pp/night

Authentic experience with onsen, tatami rooms, and incredible multi-course dinners included.

Lodge ~$40–70 pp/night

Budget-friendly with the essentials. Some include meals.

Nozawa Onsen

Ryokan ~$70–120 pp/night

Traditional inns with private onsen access. Walking distance to lifts and village.

Lodge / Apartment ~$50–90 pp/night

Self-contained apartments great for groups. Central village location.

Booking Tips

  • Book accommodation 3-6 months ahead — popular spots sell out fast
  • Look on Booking.com, Airbnb, and resort-specific booking sites
  • A chalet/lodge with a drying room is a game-changer for ski gear
  • Check if meals are included — many Japanese lodges include dinner and breakfast

Sample 7-Day Itinerary

A balanced week mixing big ski days with rest, culture, and après.

Day 1

Arrive & Settle In

Fly into Japan, transfer to resort. Check into accommodation, grab gear rentals if needed, get your bearings around town. Welcome dinner and beers with the boys.

Day 2

First Day on Snow

Ease into it — explore the mountain, find your legs. Scope out the best runs and learn the lift layout. Après beers at the base lodge.

Day 3

Full Send Day

First lifts to last lifts. Hit the steeps, find the powder stashes, explore the whole mountain. Night skiing if available (Niseko/Hakuba).

Day 4

Half Day + Culture

Morning ski session, then afternoon off. Hit the onsens, explore the village, try some local ramen or sushi. Rest those legs for the second half.

Day 5

Powder Day (Hopefully)

If the snow gods deliver, this is your day. Early start, fresh tracks, waist-deep turns. This is why you came to Japan.

Day 6

Last Ski Day

Make it count. Revisit your favourite runs, take some photos/videos, soak it all in. Big group dinner to celebrate — izakaya style.

Day 7

Depart

Pack up, transfer to airport. Optional: add a night in Tokyo or Sapporo on the way home for some city exploration and last-minute shopping.

Money Tips

  • Japan is still largely a cash society in ski towns — bring yen or use 7-Eleven ATMs
  • Food in Japan is incredible value — a bowl of ramen is ~$8-12 AUD
  • Get a Wise or Revolut card for the best exchange rates
  • Convenience stores (konbini) are your friend — cheap, delicious food 24/7
