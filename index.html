Holiday Calendar — Who's Coming When

Crowds at Japanese resorts are driven by holidays from multiple countries. Here are the key dates for the 2026/27 season.

🇬🇧

United Kingdom

  • Christmas Break: ~Dec 18 – Jan 4
  • February Half Term: Feb 15–19
  • Half term drives prices up at Niseko & Hakuba especially
🇦🇺

Australia

  • Summer Holidays: ~Dec 18 – Jan 28
  • Huge Aussie contingent in Niseko and Hakuba
  • Crowds drop significantly from late January
🇨🇳🇰🇷

China & Korea

  • Chinese New Year: Feb 6 (Golden Week Feb 6–12)
  • Korean Seollal: Feb 6–8
  • Niseko gets very crowded; Myoko & Nozawa less affected
🇯🇵

Japan

  • New Year (Oshogatsu): Dec 29 – Jan 3
  • Coming of Age Day: Jan 11
  • National Foundation Day: Feb 11
  • Weekends are always busier than weekdays
Our Pick

Jan 17–31 or Mar 7–21, 2027

The last two weeks of January hit the sweet spot — peak snowfall, Australian holidays winding down, and before Chinese New Year (Feb 6). Alternatively, early-to-mid March offers excellent value with good snow, fewer crowds, and longer sunny days — just slightly warmer temps. Aim for a Saturday-to-Saturday window for the best flight options.

The Resorts

Four world-class options, each with a different flavour. Here's how they stack up.

Niseko

Hokkaido
15m+Annual snowfall
887mVertical drop
4Linked resorts

The most famous powder destination in Japan. Four interconnected resorts (Grand Hirafu, Hanazono, Niseko Village, Annupuri) offer huge terrain variety. English-friendly with an excellent après scene.

Pros

  • Legendary consistent powder
  • Great nightlife and restaurants
  • Easy for English speakers
  • Large skiable area with night skiing

Cons

  • Most expensive option
  • Can feel less "authentically Japanese"
  • Gets crowded in peak weeks

Lift Pass

~¥7,500/day (~£37)

Hakuba

Nagano, Japanese Alps
11m+Annual snowfall
1,071mVertical drop
10Resorts in valley

Host of the 1998 Winter Olympics. Hakuba Valley has 10 resorts offering everything from steep alpine terrain to mellow groomers. A great mix of Japanese culture and international amenities.

Pros

  • Biggest vertical drop in Japan
  • Huge variety of terrain across 10 resorts
  • Good balance of culture and convenience
  • Easier access from Tokyo (bus or train)

Cons

  • Snow can be heavier/wetter than Hokkaido
  • Resorts aren't all linked — need transport between some
  • Can get windy at higher elevations

Lift Pass

~¥6,200/day (~£31)

Myoko Kogen

Niigata
14m+Annual snowfall
760mVertical drop
5Resorts in area

One of Japan's best-kept secrets. Myoko gets dumped on consistently and sees far fewer international tourists. The authentic Japanese ski town experience with incredible onsens and local food.

Pros

  • Massive snowfall — rivals Niseko
  • Way fewer crowds
  • Authentic Japanese mountain town
  • Excellent value for money

Cons

  • Limited English signage/menus
  • Smaller ski areas individually
  • Fewer nightlife options

Lift Pass

~¥5,000/day (~£25)

Nozawa Onsen

Nagano
12m+Annual snowfall
1,085mVertical drop
13Free public onsens

A charming traditional village built around natural hot springs. The skiing is excellent with great tree runs and steep terrain up top, and the village itself is one of the most atmospheric in Japan.

Pros

  • Beautiful traditional village atmosphere
  • 13 free public onsens
  • Great tree skiing and off-piste
  • Big vertical — one of Japan's largest single resorts

Cons

  • Single resort — less variety day-to-day
  • Village can be hilly to walk around
  • Limited accommodation for big groups

Lift Pass

~¥5,500/day (~£28)

Getting There

Where you fly depends on which resort you pick. Here's the breakdown.

For Niseko

Your City → New Chitose (CTS), Sapporo

Fly via Asian hubs or direct from London. From New Chitose Airport, it's a 2.5-hour bus or private transfer to Niseko.

Flights: ~£500–900 return

For Hakuba / Nozawa / Myoko

Your City → Tokyo (NRT/HND)

Fly into Tokyo, then transfer:

  • Hakuba: 4hr bus from Shinjuku or 3.5hr via bullet train + local train
  • Nozawa Onsen: 2.5hr bullet train (Hokuriku Shinkansen) to Iiyama, then 25min bus
  • Myoko Kogen: 2hr bullet train (Joetsu Shinkansen) to Joetsumyoko, then 30min bus

Flights: ~£450–800 return

Pro Tips

  • Book a JR Pass if doing Honshu resorts — covers bullet trains and saves money
  • Consider a night in Tokyo on arrival to recover from the flight before heading to the snow
  • Ski bags fly free on most airlines to Japan — check your airline's policy
  • Set up flight alerts on Google Flights or Skyscanner early for the best deals

Where to Stay

Real options for a group of 6-8, with approximate per-person-per-night costs when splitting.

Niseko

Gouka Chalet — Hirafu ~£45–50 pp/night

4-bedroom chalet in central Hirafu Village. En-suite bathrooms, ski storage, drying room. Stylish modern fit-out. Book via Elite Havens or Booking.com.

Ikigai — West Hirafu ~£40–55 pp/night

4-bedroom modern chalet (sleeps 8-10). Built 2019, open-plan living, Japanese-style bath, two-car port, dedicated dry room. Book via MnK Niseko or Powderlife.

Skye Niseko — 4 Bed Suite ~£55–75 pp/night

Premium ski-in/ski-out condo hotel. 4-bedroom corner suite with full hotel amenities and en-suite bathrooms. Book direct or via Booking.com.

Hakuba

Zen Chalets — Happo Village ~£25–45 pp/night

Modern villas with cathedral ceilings and underfloor heating. 120m walk to Happo One Gondola, surrounded by cafes and bars. 2 bedrooms + loft bunk room. Book via zenchaletshakuba.com.

Alpine Condos — Happo One ~£25–40 pp/night

True ski-in/ski-out. 3-bedroom suite (sleeps 8) with full kitchen and living area. Steps from the Sakka lifts. Great value. Book via alpine-hakuba.com.

The Castle — Echoland ~£35–55 pp/night

Largest self-contained chalet in Hakuba Valley. 8 bedrooms, 9 en-suite bathrooms, games room with pool table. Near Echoland bars and restaurants. Book via Hakuba Hospitality Group.

Myoko Kogen

Rider's Ridge — Akakura Onsen ~£35–40 pp/night

Newly renovated luxury chalet, ski-in/ski-out overlooking Akakura Onsen Ski Field. 5 bedrooms, sleeps 12. Valley views, laundry, drying room. Book via Wasabi Chalets or Booking.com.

Myoko Ski Lodge — Akakura ~£20–30 pp/night

Western-style lodge, 3-minute walk to slopes. Continental breakfast included, natural onsen hot springs on-site. Great value base. Book via myokoskilodge.com.

Kitsune Lodge — Ikenotaira ~£25–35 pp/night

Stylish boutique lodge with 9 bedrooms, wood fireplace, communal spaces. Perfect for exclusive group bookings. Book via kitsunemyoko.com.

Nozawa Onsen

Nozawa Gondola Apartments ~£60–70 pp/night

Luxury 3-bedroom apartments just 20m from the slopes, 30m to main gondola. Full kitchens, en-suite bathrooms, jacuzzi access. Book via nozawaholidays.com.

Kamoshika Ski Lodge ~£45–60 pp/night

Luxury renovated ski-in/ski-out lodge next to Nagasaka Gondola. 5 en-suite bedrooms, sleeps up to 16. Premium finishes throughout. Book via staynozawa.com.

Sakaya Hanare Jon Nobi ~£40–55 pp/night

Modern apartments by renowned Ryokan Sakaya. 3 twin bedrooms + loft, full kitchen, jacuzzi. 2-min walk to lifts via moving walkway. Book via ryokan-sakaya.co.jp.

Booking Tips

  • Book accommodation 3-6 months ahead — popular spots sell out fast
  • Look on Booking.com, Airbnb, and resort-specific booking sites listed above
  • A chalet/lodge with a drying room is a game-changer for ski gear
  • Check if meals are included — many Japanese lodges include dinner and breakfast
  • Myoko and Hakuba offer the best value — Niseko and Nozawa are pricier but very polished

Sample 7-Day Itinerary

A balanced week mixing big ski days with rest, culture, and après.

Day 1

Arrive & Settle In

Fly into Japan, transfer to resort. Check into accommodation, grab gear rentals if needed, get your bearings around town. Welcome dinner and beers with the boys.

Day 2

First Day on Snow

Ease into it — explore the mountain, find your legs. Scope out the best runs and learn the lift layout. Après beers at the base lodge.

Day 3

Full Send Day

First lifts to last lifts. Hit the steeps, find the powder stashes, explore the whole mountain. Night skiing if available (Niseko/Hakuba).

Day 4

Half Day + Culture

Morning ski session, then afternoon off. Hit the onsens, explore the village, try some local ramen or sushi. Rest those legs for the second half.

Day 5

Powder Day (Hopefully)

If the snow gods deliver, this is your day. Early start, fresh tracks, waist-deep turns. This is why you came to Japan.

Day 6

Last Ski Day

Make it count. Revisit your favourite runs, take some photos/videos, soak it all in. Big group dinner to celebrate — izakaya style.

Day 7

Depart

Pack up, transfer to airport. Optional: add a night in Tokyo or Sapporo on the way home for some city exploration and last-minute shopping.

Money Tips

  • Japan is still largely a cash society in ski towns — bring yen or use 7-Eleven ATMs
  • Food in Japan is incredible value — a bowl of ramen is ~£4-6
  • Get a Wise or Revolut card for the best exchange rates
  • Convenience stores (konbini) are your friend — cheap, delicious food 24/7
